      Position of vertices of Archimedean solids on their Platonic parents:
Midpoint Truncation: Choose the midpoints of the Platonic edges as the vertices of a new polyhedron. From the tetrahedron we get the octahedron. From cube or octahedron we get the cuboctahedron which has 6 square faces and 8 triangular faces. From icosahedron or dodecahedron we get the icosidodecahedron which has 12 pentagon faces and 20 triangular faces.
Standard Truncation: Cut the k-edged vertices so that the Platonic n-gon faces become regular 2n-gons and new k-gon faces appear below the Platonic vertices. Names: truncated tetrahedron, truncated octahedron, truncated cube, truncated icosahedron, truncated dodecahedron.
Edge Truncation: Scale each Platonic n-face from its midpoint down and take the vertices of these smaller n-gons as the vertices of a new polyhedron. Below the k-edged vertices appear regular k-gon faces and below the edges appear rectangular faces. With the correct scaling these rectangles are squares. From the tetrahedron we get the cuboctahedron. From cube or octahedron we get the rhombicuboctahedron which has (6+12) square faces and 8 triangular faces. From dodecahedron or icosahedron we get the rhombicosidodecahedron which has 12 pentagon faces, 20 triangular faces and 30 square faces.
Snub Truncation: Scale the Platonic n-faces down as before but also rotate them around their midpoints and take the vertices of these smaller n-gons as the vertices of a new polyhedron. Again we get k-gon faces below the k-edged vertices and below each edge appear two triangular faces. One can adjust the rotation parameter so that all these triangles are isoceles; with the correct scaling they are also equilateral. From the tetrahedron one gets the icosahedron. From cube or octahedron one gets the snub cube which has 6 square faces and (8+24) triangular faces. From dodecahedron or icosahedron one gets the snub dodecahedron which has 12 pentagon faces and (20+60) triangular faces. These two snub polyhedra are different from their mirror images.
Shrink Truncation: The two remaining Archimedean solids are often described as a modified vertex truncation of the cuboctahedron and the icosidodecahedron. It is maybe simpler to start from the standard truncation above, shrink the regular 2n-gon faces from their midpoints and take the vertices of all these smaller 2n-gons as the vertices of a new polyhedron. As with edge truncation we get rectangles under the edges; these are made squares with the correct scaling. Under the k-edged old vertices we get new 2k-gon faces. The tetrahedron thus gives the truncated octahedron (4 squares and 8 hexagons). Cube or octahedron give the truncated cuboctahedron (6 eight-gons, 8 hexagons and 12 squares). Dodecahedron or icosahedron give the truncated icosidodecahedron (12 ten-gons, 20 hexagons and 30 squares).
